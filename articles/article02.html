Countryside and Cosmic Horror: an inevitable contrast.

Countryside life reminds me of inner peace, simple life, traditions, and simple — but harsh — rewarding tasks.

"[...] It was everywhere — a gelatin —a slime—yet it had shapes, a thousand shapes of horror beyond all memory. There were eyes — and a blemish."

- "The Unnamable" by H.P. Lovecraft.

Cosmic Horror is all about realizing how small and vulnerable you are as a human. Imagine yourself, on an open field, looking up at the night sky, staring at the stars, glancing at that sudden sparkle that it emits. The vastness of that black/purplish emptiness space starts growing on your mind. Now, you are not afraid of being alone but, actually, of not being alone at all. Nothing is more frightening compared to that specific moment when you realize the vastness of the universe.

The contrast is what makes things being meaningful as themselves and, it's all about showing its core features. The high contrast between Countryside and Cosmic Horror can create a unique ambiance and, that's the main concern while creating the Countrysiding's world — a.k.a. Planet X-19.

I think you might be asking yourself how I am approaching this. I'll bring more details in the future but, it's all about creating the right atmosphere. A game experience doesn't limit itself only on the creations of its developer but also on the player's interpretation — to achieve what I'm looking for, I can't deny the player's creativity.

Character Development: creating real-life resemblance.

Characters are what give life to a story. When creating a game similar to Stardew Valley, Harvest Moon, it's unquestionable that character development is one of the most important aspects to focus on during your early development. In this case, without your characters, you'll not even have a game.

Game mechanics on RPGs like this have — or should have — strict relations with the characters. An NPC must feel unreplaceable and, its characteristics need to make it feel unique but recognizable — the player needs to sympathize with the ideas, thoughts, and feelings.

The player's character in games like this needs to represent the players or, at least, whom they want to be. That's why I create a simple backstory for the player's character. Due to the backstory simplicity, I'll add meaningful multi-choice situations that make a real difference in the story gameplay and other elements.

Now, I'll present a brief and self-explanatory description of a character that has significant importance on the gameplay, currently under development: Oswald, the Astronomer.

"Oswald is a renowned scientist from Earth that traveled and moved to Planet X-19 to learn and study about different and unusual celestial bodies. He informs the local farmers about the weather, seasons, and weird phenomena. But, somehow, he knows more information than science can explain. Well respected by the community, he is one of the most important figures of Moonriver Town."

A bit too practical and informative, what about some classic countryside drama? Jolene is one of the most eccentric characters and might bring some drama alongside the mayor's gossip wife, Ma'am Fynn.

"Jolene saw her marriage fall apart after her husband abandoned her because of her pregnancy. She saw him leaving through their old house's door and never coming back. Things got even harder after her sister died during labor — her sister was already a widow. She needed to take care of the newborn niece named after her sister: Victoria. Luckily, she received as an inheritance her sister's farm. Almost, harsh twenty years, the "Countrysiding" program seemed to be the only solution after losing her farm for the bank due to a lot of unmanageable debts."

Each character will have a backstory that may be part of something bigger or not. Characters may participate in others' stories and, not necessarily, have one big plot just for itself.

Game Design and Art: decisions.

In my opinion, deciding on what art style your game will have is an irreversible decision. This decision starts when you choose the technique you will use, and it'll not stop there. This decision has a lot of nuances and little things that will make your life, as a designer, miserable — but, in the end, it's worth it.

There is an undebatable relation between game design and art: art helps transcribe the feelings that your game design wants to deliver. Every single part of your game is affected by the game design, and one of the ways to show it for the players is using art.

That's why I decided to go along with Pixel Art. The mood, the simplicity — yet nostalgic —, the unrealistic aspect, and other characteristics make it perfect for the game. The specific art style is Isometric Pixel Art — a not so simple approach but works interestingly with lightning and allows the creation of uncommon scenarios.

The art under development is on a very alpha stage, and it's not ready for exposition. Soon it'll be here.

Overwhelm? Proseche!

As I already said: I will not rush anything during the Countrysiding development. I'm learning. I'm making mistakes. I'm relearning. I'm developing.

Currently, I decided to go way back and relearn the art fundamentals, and it is being a total blast. I'm developing my skills as an artist, and I can feel it — and see it.

Alongside the art studies, I'm currently reading the following books: "Think Like a Programmer" by V. Anton Spraul; "The Grasshopper: Games, Life, and Utopia" by Bernard Suits; "Level Up: The Guide to Great Video Game Design" by Scott Rogers.

To enrich my creativity, I'm reading some of the classic tales by H. P. Lovecraft and "The Silmarillion" by J. R. R. Tolkien. Later, I might start reading tales written by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle and Edgar Allan Poe.

Oh, yes, almost forgot. Also, I'm developing my skills as a musician and composer doing little music pieces on Cakewalk — not worthy of being shown. Now, my final message.

In one of my introspective moments, I thought about the relationship between a game developer and an artist/designer. We, as game developers, must understand that we are, first of all, ultimate artists. There is no other way that allows us to show our art so complete than with video games. It's all feelings and sensations tied together by one piece of art.

So: if your art doesn't come from the bottom of your heart, it's not worth anyone's time; create something that will enable you how to express and present your Weltanschauung; create something that fulfills the emptiness of a rushed daily life; create something that will impact others as it impacts you. Create art!
